Definition & Time-Domain Expression

We begin with a sinusoidal carrier signal:

$$c(t) = A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

and a baseband message signal $m(t)$ with bandwidth $W$. The standard AM signal is formed by varying the carrier amplitude linearly with $m(t)$:

Key Result — AM Signal
$$s_{AM}(t) = A_c \left[1 + k_a m(t)\right]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$$
The carrier envelope follows the message signal, shifted up by a DC level of 1.

For a sinusoidal message $m(t) = A_m \cos(2\pi f_m t)$, we define the modulation index:

$$\mu = k_a A_m$$
Derivation: Modulation Index and Envelope Condition

Substituting the sinusoidal message into the AM expression:

$$s_{AM}(t) = A_c [1 + \mu \cos(2\pi f_m t)] \cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

The envelope of this signal is:

$$E(t) = A_c |1 + \mu \cos(2\pi f_m t)|$$

For the envelope to faithfully reproduce $m(t)$ (no phase reversals), we require:

$$1 + \mu \cos(2\pi f_m t) \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } t$$

Since $\cos(2\pi f_m t)$ achieves a minimum of $-1$, this requires $1 - \mu \geq 0$, i.e.,

$$\boxed{0 \leq \mu \leq 1}$$

When $\mu > 1$ (overmodulation), the envelope passes through zero and the signal undergoes phase reversals. An envelope detector cannot recover $m(t)$ without distortion in this regime.

Spectrum & Bandwidth

Taking the Fourier transform of $s_{AM}(t)$ for single-tone modulation:

Derivation: Fourier Transform of AM Signal
Step 1 — Expand the product

$$s_{AM}(t) = A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t) + \frac{\mu A_c}{2}\cos[2\pi(f_c + f_m)t] + \frac{\mu A_c}{2}\cos[2\pi(f_c - f_m)t]$$

Step 2 — Fourier transform

$$S_{AM}(f) = \frac{A_c}{2}[\delta(f - f_c) + \delta(f + f_c)]$$ $$\quad + \frac{\mu A_c}{4}[\delta(f - f_c - f_m) + \delta(f + f_c + f_m)]$$ $$\quad + \frac{\mu A_c}{4}[\delta(f - f_c + f_m) + \delta(f + f_c - f_m)]$$

Step 3 — Identify components

The spectrum consists of:

  • Carrier component at $\pm f_c$ with amplitude $A_c/2$
  • Upper sideband (USB) at $\pm(f_c + f_m)$ with amplitude $\mu A_c / 4$
  • Lower sideband (LSB) at $\pm(f_c - f_m)$ with amplitude $\mu A_c / 4$
Key Result — AM Bandwidth
$$B_T = 2W$$
The total transmission bandwidth is twice the message bandwidth — the price of double-sideband modulation.

Power Analysis

Derivation: Total Power and Efficiency
Step 1 — Carrier power

$$P_c = \frac{A_c^2}{2}$$

Step 2 — Sideband power (single tone)

Each sideband contributes power $\frac{\mu^2 A_c^2}{8}$, so total sideband power:

$$P_s = 2 \times \frac{\mu^2 A_c^2}{8} = \frac{\mu^2 A_c^2}{4} = \frac{\mu^2}{2} P_c$$
Step 3 — Total power
$$P_T = P_c + P_s = P_c\left(1 + \frac{\mu^2}{2}\right)$$
Step 4 — Efficiency

Only the sideband power carries information:

$$\eta = \frac{P_s}{P_T} = \frac{\mu^2/2}{1 + \mu^2/2}$$

For $\mu = 1$: $\eta = \frac{1/2}{3/2} = \frac{1}{3} \approx 33.3\%$

Key Result — AM Efficiency
$$\eta = \frac{\mu^2/2}{1 + \mu^2/2}$$
At best (μ = 1), only 33.3% of transmitted power carries information — the rest is wasted in the carrier.

Generation Methods

Square-Law Modulator

A nonlinear device with transfer characteristic $v_o = a_1 v_i + a_2 v_i^2$ is fed the sum $v_i(t) = A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t) + m(t)$.

Expanding:

$$v_o(t) = a_1 A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t) + a_1 m(t) + a_2 A_c^2 \cos^2(2\pi f_c t) + 2a_2 A_c m(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) + a_2 m^2(t)$$

A bandpass filter centered at $f_c$ extracts:

$$s(t) = a_1 A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t) + 2a_2 A_c m(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) = a_1 A_c\left[1 + \frac{2a_2}{a_1} m(t)\right]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

This is a standard AM signal with $k_a = 2a_2/a_1$.

Switching Modulator

A diode switches periodically at the carrier frequency. The switching waveform has a Fourier series:

$$g_T(t) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{2}{\pi}\cos(2\pi f_c t) - \frac{2}{3\pi}\cos(6\pi f_c t) + \cdots$$

The product $v(t) = [A_c + m(t)] \cdot g_T(t)$ produces a component at $f_c$:

$$\frac{2}{\pi}[A_c + m(t)]\cos(2\pi f_c t) = \frac{2A_c}{\pi}\left[1 + \frac{m(t)}{A_c}\right]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

After bandpass filtering, this is AM with $k_a = 1/A_c$.

Balanced Modulator → DSB-SC

Two AM modulators produce $s_1(t) = A_c[1 + k_a m(t)]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$ and $s_2(t) = A_c[1 - k_a m(t)]\cos(2\pi f_c t)$.

Subtracting:

$$s_1(t) - s_2(t) = 2A_c k_a m(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

The carrier is suppressed — this is DSB-SC modulation.

Demodulation

Envelope Detector

A diode followed by an RC circuit tracks the envelope of the AM signal. The RC time constant must satisfy:

$$\frac{1}{f_c} \ll RC \ll \frac{1}{W}$$
Fast enough to follow the envelope, slow enough to smooth out the carrier.

If $RC$ is too small, carrier ripple appears. If $RC$ is too large, the output cannot follow rapid envelope changes (diagonal clipping).

Synchronous (Coherent) Detector

Multiply the received signal by a local oscillator $\cos(2\pi f_c t + \phi)$ and low-pass filter:

$$y(t) = \text{LPF}\{s_{AM}(t) \cos(2\pi f_c t + \phi)\}$$ $$= \frac{A_c}{2}[1 + k_a m(t)] \cos\phi$$

For $\phi = 0$ (perfect synchronization), the output is proportional to $m(t)$. Phase error $\phi$ causes amplitude scaling by $\cos\phi$.

Square-Law Demodulator

Square the received signal:

$$s_{AM}^2(t) = A_c^2[1 + k_a m(t)]^2 \cos^2(2\pi f_c t)$$ $$= \frac{A_c^2}{2}[1 + k_a m(t)]^2 [1 + \cos(4\pi f_c t)]$$

LPF removes the $2f_c$ component:

$$y(t) = \frac{A_c^2}{2}[1 + 2k_a m(t) + k_a^2 m^2(t)]$$

The $m^2(t)$ term causes distortion. For small $k_a$: $k_a^2 m^2(t) \ll 2k_a m(t)$, distortion is negligible.

Figure of Merit — SNR Comparison

Derivation: Output SNR for AM, DSB-SC, and SSB

Using the noise analysis framework with input SNR $\gamma = S_T / (N_0 B_T)$:

AM (envelope detection)
$$\left(\frac{S}{N}\right)_o = \frac{\mu^2/2}{1 + \mu^2/2} \cdot \gamma \quad (\text{above threshold})$$
DSB-SC (coherent)
$$\left(\frac{S}{N}\right)_o = 2\gamma$$
SSB (coherent)
$$\left(\frac{S}{N}\right)_o = \gamma$$

The figure of merit (FOM) is the ratio of output SNR to baseband SNR:

SchemeFOMBW
AM (μ=1)1/32W
DSB-SC12W
SSB1W
