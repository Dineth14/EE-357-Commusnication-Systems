PCM Overview

Pulse Code Modulation (PCM) is the standard method for digital representation of analog signals. The process involves three steps: sampling, quantization, and encoding.

$$\text{Bit rate} = f_s \times B \text{ bits/second}$$

where $f_s$ is the sampling rate and $B$ is bits per sample. For telephone-quality audio: $f_s = 8000$ Hz, $B = 8$ → 64 kbps (DS-0 channel).

Encoding Process

  1. Sampling: Convert continuous signal to discrete samples at rate $f_s \geq 2f_m$
  2. Quantization: Map each sample to nearest of $L = 2^B$ levels
  3. Encoding: Represent each quantized level as a $B$-bit binary codeword
$$\text{Minimum bandwidth}=\frac{R_b}{2}=\frac{f_s \cdot B}{2} \text{ Hz (Nyquist BW)}$$

Companding

Uniform quantization gives poor SQNR for small signals. Companding (compress + expand) applies a non-linear compression before quantization and the inverse expansion after decoding.

μ-Law (North America, Japan)

$$y = F_\mu(x) = \text{sgn}(x)\frac{\ln(1 + \mu|x|)}{\ln(1 + \mu)}, \quad \mu = 255$$

A-Law (Europe, International)

$$y = F_A(x) = \text{sgn}(x)\begin{cases}\frac{A|x|}{1+\ln A} & |x|<\frac{1}{A}\\ \frac{1+\ln(A|x|)}{1+\ln A} & \frac{1}{A}\leq|x|\leq 1\end{cases}, \quad A=87.6$$
Companding SNR improvement

With companding, the SQNR becomes approximately independent of signal level for large $\mu$ or $A$:

$$\text{SQNR}_{\mu\text{-law}} \approx \frac{3L^2}{[\ln(1+\mu)]^2}$$

Compared to uniform: $\text{SQNR}_{uniform} = \frac{3L^2}{2}$ at full scale, but degrades as $\propto x^2$ for small signals. Companding equalizes SQNR across all signal levels.

Differential PCM (DPCM)

DPCM encodes the difference between successive samples rather than absolute values:

$$e(n) = m(n) - \hat{m}(n)$$

where $\hat{m}(n)$ is a prediction based on past samples. Since differences are typically smaller, fewer bits are needed → lower bit rate. A simple first-order predictor: $\hat{m}(n) = m(n-1)$.

Delta Modulation (DM)

DM is a 1-bit DPCM: each sample is encoded as a single bit indicating whether the signal increased or decreased:

$$\hat{m}(n) = \hat{m}(n-1) + \delta \cdot \text{sgn}[m(n) - \hat{m}(n-1)]$$

where $\delta$ is the step size. Two types of distortion:

  • Slope overload: Signal changes faster than $\delta \cdot f_s$ → requires $\delta > \frac{2\pi f_m A_m}{f_s}$
  • Granular noise: Signal is nearly flat but staircase oscillates by $\pm\delta$

Adaptive DM (ADM) varies $\delta$ dynamically to balance both distortion types.

Time-Division Multiplexing (TDM)

TDM interleaves samples from multiple channels into a single bitstream:

$$R_{total} = N \times f_s \times B \text{ bps}$$

where $N$ is the number of channels. T1 System: 24 channels × 8 kHz × 8 bits + 1 framing bit = 1.544 Mbps. E1: 32 channels × 8 kHz × 8 bits = 2.048 Mbps.
