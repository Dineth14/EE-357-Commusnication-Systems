Introduction

In angle modulation, information is carried in the angle (instantaneous phase or frequency) of the carrier, while the envelope remains constant. This provides improved noise immunity at the expense of bandwidth — a powerful trade-off exploited by FM radio, telemetry, and many digital systems.

The general angle-modulated signal is:

$$s(t)=A_c\cos[\theta_i(t)]$$

where $\theta_i(t)$ is the instantaneous angle. The instantaneous frequency is:

$$f_i(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{d\theta_i(t)}{dt}$$

Phase Modulation (PM)

In PM, the instantaneous phase is directly proportional to the message signal:

$$\theta_i(t)=2\pi f_c t + k_p\,m(t)$$

where $k_p$ is the phase sensitivity in radians per volt. Thus:

$$s_{PM}(t)=A_c\cos\!\bigl[2\pi f_c t + k_p\,m(t)\bigr]$$

The maximum phase deviation is $\Delta\phi=k_p\max|m(t)|$. For a single-tone message $m(t)=A_m\cos(2\pi f_m t)$, the peak deviation is $\Delta\phi=k_p A_m$.

Derivation: PM instantaneous frequency

Starting from $\theta_i(t)=2\pi f_c t + k_p A_m\cos(2\pi f_m t)$:

$$f_i(t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{d\theta_i}{dt}=f_c - \frac{k_p A_m f_m}{1}\sin(2\pi f_m t)$$

Maximum frequency deviation: $\Delta f = k_p A_m f_m$. Notice that in PM, frequency deviation depends on both the amplitude and frequency of the message.

Frequency Modulation (FM)

In FM, the instantaneous frequency is directly proportional to the message:

$$f_i(t)=f_c + k_f\,m(t)$$

where $k_f$ is the frequency sensitivity in Hz/V. The instantaneous phase is obtained by integration:

$$\theta_i(t)=2\pi f_c t + 2\pi k_f\int_{0}^{t}m(\tau)\,d\tau$$

The FM signal is therefore:

$$s_{FM}(t)=A_c\cos\!\left[2\pi f_c t + 2\pi k_f\int_{0}^{t}m(\tau)\,d\tau\right]$$

For a single-tone message $m(t)=A_m\cos(2\pi f_m t)$:

$$s_{FM}(t)=A_c\cos\!\bigl[2\pi f_c t + \beta\sin(2\pi f_m t)\bigr]$$

where the modulation index is:

$$\beta=\frac{\Delta f}{f_m}=\frac{k_f A_m}{f_m}$$

Bessel Function Expansion

Using the Jacobi-Anger identity, the single-tone FM signal expands as:

$$s_{FM}(t)=A_c\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}J_n(\beta)\cos\!\bigl[2\pi(f_c+n f_m)t\bigr]$$

where $J_n(\beta)$ is the Bessel function of the first kind, order $n$. Key properties:

  • $J_{-n}(\beta)=(-1)^n J_n(\beta)$
  • $\displaystyle\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}J_n^2(\beta)=1$ (constant power, since envelope is constant)
  • For $\beta\ll 1$: $J_0(\beta)\approx 1$, $J_1(\beta)\approx\beta/2$, $J_n(\beta)\approx 0$ for $|n|\ge 2$ → NBFM
Derivation: Jacobi-Anger identity

We start with the complex exponential form:

$$e^{j\beta\sin\theta}=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}J_n(\beta)\,e^{jn\theta}$$

Using Euler's formula for the FM signal:

$$s(t)=\text{Re}\{A_c\,e^{j(2\pi f_c t+\beta\sin 2\pi f_m t)}\}=\text{Re}\left\{A_c\,e^{j2\pi f_c t}\sum_n J_n(\beta)\,e^{jn2\pi f_m t}\right\}$$

Taking the real part yields the cosine expansion above.

Carson's Rule

Although FM is theoretically infinite in bandwidth, Carson's rule gives a practical estimate encompassing ~98% of the signal power:

$$B_T\approx 2(\Delta f + f_m)=2f_m(\beta + 1)$$

For NBFM ($\beta\ll 1$): $B_T\approx 2f_m$ (same as AM). For WBFM ($\beta\gg 1$): $B_T\approx 2\Delta f$.

FM Generation Methods

Direct Method — VCO

A Voltage-Controlled Oscillator produces a frequency proportional to the input voltage. Simple but may suffer from carrier frequency drift.

Indirect Method — Armstrong

Armstrong's method generates NBFM first, then uses frequency multiplication to widen $\beta$ while keeping the carrier stable with a crystal oscillator:

  1. Generate NBFM at low carrier frequency $f_1$ with small $\beta_1$
  2. Apply frequency multiplier ($\times n$): new $\beta=n\beta_1$, new $f_c=n f_1$
  3. Use mixer + another multiplier to reach desired $f_c$ and $\beta$
$$\text{After }n\text{-fold multiplication: }\;\beta_{out}=n\,\beta_{in},\quad f_{c,out}=n\,f_{c,in}$$

FM Demodulation

Frequency Discriminator

Converts frequency variations to amplitude variations using a circuit whose output is proportional to the input frequency deviation. Consists of a differentiator followed by an envelope detector:

$$v_o(t)\propto \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[\theta_i(t)\bigr]\propto f_i(t)-f_c \propto m(t)$$

Phase-Locked Loop (PLL)

Tracks the instantaneous phase of the FM signal. The VCO control voltage is proportional to $m(t)$:

  1. Phase detector compares input FM signal with VCO output
  2. Loop filter smooths the error signal
  3. VCO frequency tracks $f_i(t)$; its control voltage $\propto m(t)$

Ratio Detector & Foster-Seeley

Balanced discriminator circuits using tuned transformers — historically used in analog FM receivers. The ratio detector provides inherent amplitude limiting.

SNR Analysis

For FM with single-tone modulation and additive white Gaussian noise:

$$\text{SNR}_o=3\beta^2(\beta+1)\cdot\text{SNR}_{baseband}$$

This is the wideband FM advantage — trading bandwidth for improved SNR. The improvement of $3\beta^2(\beta+1)$ over baseband is massive for large $\beta$.

Derivation: FM output SNR

For FM with a single-tone message of bandwidth $W=f_m$:

$$\text{SNR}_o=\frac{3A_c^2 k_f^2 A_m^2}{8\pi^2 N_0 W^3}=\frac{3\beta^2(\beta+1)}{1}\cdot\frac{A_c^2}{2N_0 B_T}\cdot\frac{B_T}{W}$$

Compared to baseband SNR ($= A_c^2/(2N_0 W)$), the FM gain is $3\beta^2(\beta+1)$.

Pre-emphasis / De-emphasis: Using a pre-emphasis filter ($H(f)\propto f$) at the transmitter and a complementary de-emphasis filter at the receiver provides an additional gain of $(f_m/f_1)^2/3$ for triangular noise spectrum suppression.

Threshold effect: Below a certain input SNR ($\approx 10$ dB), FM performance degrades rapidly due to spike noise (clicks).

FM Threshold: When $\text{SNR}_{in}$ falls below approximately 10 dB, the demodulator performance degrades severely due to impulse noise events (clicks). PLL demodulators have lower threshold (~3 dB below discriminator).
